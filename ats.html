Spiderman: Across the Spider-Verse

About the Movie

Spiderman: Across the Spider-Verse is the sequel to Spiderman: Into the Spider-Verse, which introduced Miles Morales, the second spiderman to exist in his dimension. Shortly after gaining his powers, a rift in the multiverse opens up, and Miles meets several other "spider-people" from other dimensions. The sequel picks up where the first movie left off, with all the spider-people back in their original dimension and Miles is missing his other-dimensional friends. He is struggling to fit in at school and is having trouble balancing his life as a superhero with his life as a student. Miles is one day surprised to see Gwen appearing through a portal in his room. He is even more surprised to hear that she has joined an "elite force" of spider-people who can travel between dimensions without the risk of glitching thanks to their watches. Miles is disappointed when Gwen refuses to let him go with her on her interdimensional missions and he soon discovers that there is a much darker side to the "elite force" than Gwen let on.

Animation

The Spider-Verse film franchise has been trail-blazing new animation techniques from the get-go. These movies are another stellar example of combining 3D and 2D elements to make visually incredible films. The first movie incporated so many revolutionary techniques, such as using Ben-Day dots throughout most of the artwork to give it the same aesthetic as a comic book. Like Puss in Boots: The Last Wish, these films incorporate different frame rates to emphasize certain elements. In the first film while Miles is still getting used to his new powers, he is animated on twos while Peter B. Parker, who has already perfected his craft, is animated on ones. This makes Miles' movements look less refined and smooth. In Across the Spider-Verse, the animators took this technique to the next level and decided to animate another character, Hobie, on threes. Rather than making his movements look less practiced like in the first movie, Hobie's clearly 'zine and graffiti-inspired design resulted in his movements looking far more 2D than the other characters and evoked a very paper-like texture.

Social Implications

There are several socially impactful elements to this film. The first film was obviously impactful in its featuring a black-latino superhero. One of the most notable elements of the second film is a background element that we see in Gwen's bedroom, a trans pride flag that reads "Protect Trans Kids." Consequently, many fans believe that Gwen herself is trans and she has become something of a trans icon. This is further emphasized by the colour palette of her super suit very closely matching the trans flag. The film features many other diverse characters in terms of race, disability, sexuality, and gender. It is a wonderful example of representation in animation and has already resonated with so many fans.
